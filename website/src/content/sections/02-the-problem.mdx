## The Algorithmic Problem

Inference autoscaling is a **sequential decision problem** under uncertainty. At each timestep you observe traffic and cluster state, choose whether to add, hold, or remove capacity, and pay for that choice over the next interval.

### Cost vs Latency

Every policy sits somewhere on a **Pareto frontier** between:

- **Cost** — proportional to active replicas (GPUs, pods, or nodes)
- **Latency** — overload when arrival rate exceeds capacity, plus queue backlog and dropped work

Policies that minimize cost alone leave the cluster under-provisioned during spikes. Policies that chase minimum latency over-provision and waste budget.

### Why simple baselines struggle

| Baseline | Behavior | Failure Mode |
|----------|----------|-------------------------------|
| **Do nothing** | Never scales | Catastrophic overload; worst latency |
| **Fixed replicas** | Constant capacity | Can't follow spikes or troughs |
| **Target utilization (HPA-style)** | React to utilization | Lag behind bursts and oscillates |
| **Greedy** | Scale from next-step demand | Ignores cold start; poor on replay |

This benchmark on a **200-step GPU-style traffic trace** shows orders-of-magnitude gaps in episode return between RL policies and static baselines along with a surprising gap between **greedy** and **learned** policies once boot delay and queueing matter.

The evaluation trace is synthetic but designed to mimic GPU inference serving behavior: periods of moderate load, sudden request bursts, and quiet intervals where excess replicas become expensive. 
This makes it useful for testing cold-start-aware autoscaling.

The important failure mode is that the right replica count at time $t$ may still be too late if new replicas take several steps to become available.
