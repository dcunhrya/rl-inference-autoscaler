## Introduction

Serving large language models and other GPU-backed ML workloads at scale is a tradeoff. 
On one side, we need enough GPU replicas to absorb **unpredictable traffic spikes**. 
On the other, every extra **idle replica is expensive**.

Most production systems rely on **rule-based autoscaling**. 
Kubernetes Horizontal Pod Autoscaler scales replicas based on signals like average CPU utilization. 
These heuristics are simple and reliable, but they are **reactive**. 
They work when load changes gradually, but batch jobs and sudden request bursts can all push demand up faster than new GPU workers can **cold start**.
If replicas lag behind demand, requests queue, latency rises, and service-level objectives can break. 

Rather than hand-designing a fixed scaling rule, we ask whether an RL policy can **learn when to scale GPU replicas**.
In this post, we build a **Gymnasium** environment that simulates request rate, GPU utilization, replica count, cold starts, and queueing behavior. 
We train **PPO** and **DQN** autoscaling policies with **Ray RLlib** and compare them against baselines. 
The goal is to understand when learned scaling policies can **trade off latency and cost** better than fixed rules.
