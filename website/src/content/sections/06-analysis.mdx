## Analysis

### Why PPO beats greedy on return

Greedy minimizes **instantaneous** mismatch between RPS and capacity but does not account for **cold-start delay**: scaling up today does not add throughput for several timesteps. 
Greedy accumulates a **large latency penalty** (17,468 mean) despite low replica cost (98).

PPO and DQN learn to **anticipate** bursts from state features (RPS, utilization, delta) and accept higher replica cost during volatile windows which reduces overload without a perfect oracle.

### Ground-truth gap (oracle replicas)

Scaling plots compare **active replicas** to an **ideal** count derived from instantaneous RPS (no boot delay). 
Symmetric \|ΔN\| (where ΔN = active − ideal) alone is misleading as **underprovisioning** (ideal > active) drives SLO risk while **overprovisioning** (active > ideal) mainly adds cost. 
Mean gaps from results:

| Policy | Under (SLO risk) | Over (extra cost) |
|--------|------------------|-------------------|
| Greedy | 1.12 | 0.58 |
| PPO | 0.05 | 9.56 |
| DQN | 0.08 | 7.78 |

Greedy stays near ideal in replica space yet still loses on **return** because cold-start delay means matching ideal count is not enough to clear overload immediately.

### Why PPO overprovisions but DQN tracks ideal more closely

On the scaling figure above, **PPO** often sits at **max replicas** after bursts and is slow to scale down (“delayed scale-down”), while **DQN** follows the **ground truth** more closely. **Greedy** underprovisions spikes but replicates correctly.

This is not a training failure! The two algorithms learn **different points on the same reward tradeoff**:

1. **PPO minimizes latency first.** With `latency_beta` much larger than replica cost (`cost_alpha`), PPO learns that **missing capacity during cold-start** is expensive. 
It scales up aggressively (122 scale-up vs 47 scale-down actions on this episode) and **holds high replica counts** through quiet periods so it never re-enters overload when traffic returns. 
That shows up as **mean over ≈ 9.6** and **mean under ≈ 0.05**.
Almost no SLO risk, lots of extra GPUs (**high cost**).

2. **DQN scales down more often.** On the same trace, DQN takes **84 scale-down** steps vs PPO’s 47, so it **releases capacity sooner** when utilization falls. Replica counts stay closer to ideal (**mean over ≈ 7.8**, **mean under ≈ 0.08**), which looks better against ground truth but leaves slightly more headroom for queue buildup (**latency 639** vs PPO’s **393**).

3. **On-policy vs off-policy dynamics.** 
PPO updates from the policy it just ran.
Once “scale up early, scale down late” works, the clipped objective **reinforces** that safe-but-expensive behavior. 
DQN’s replay and Q-learning spread credit across many past states, which tends to produce a **less extreme** replica level (closer to greedy in shape) but still with enough proactive scale-up to beat greedy on return.

**Takeaway:** PPO wins **episode return** (−692 vs −902) by paying for **reliability** (low underprovisioning). 
DQN wins **visual fit to ideal** by accepting a bit more SLO risk and less idle capacity. 
Neither metric is good enough to justify deploying one algorithm. Compare return, latency/cost penalties, and asymmetric under/over gaps together.

### Best Algorithm for Deployment (For Now)

For deployment, based on current results, PPO is the stronger default from these benchmarks. DQN remains useful as an off-policy baseline and for ablations (learning rate, replay, etc.).
