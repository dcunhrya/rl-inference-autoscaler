Introduction

Serving large language models and other ML workloads at scale means paying for GPU replicas that must track unpredictable traffic. Kubernetes Horizontal Pod Autoscaler (HPA) reacts to average CPU or custom metrics with fixed rules. That works for steady load, but inference spikes (batch jobs, product launches, diurnal patterns) often arrive faster than cold-start delays allow.

When replicas lag behind demand, requests can queue, latency can spikes, and SLOs can break. When you over-provision, GPUs sit idle and cloud bills climb (which nowdays costs a fortune). The trade-off is inherently multi-objective: minimize cost and minimize latency under uncertainty.

My investigation focuses on the following question: can a reinforcement learning policy learn to scale inference replicas proactively from cluster metrics, outperforming classical heuristics in a realistic simulator?

I built a Gymnasium environment that models request rate, utilization, replica count, cold starts, and queueing; trained PPO and DQN policies with Ray RLlib; and benchmarked them against baselines on a GPU-style synthetic traffic trace.

The Algorithmic Problem

Inference autoscaling is a sequential decision problem under uncertainty. At each timestep you observe traffic and cluster state, choose whether to add, hold, or remove capacity, and pay for that choice over the next interval.

Cost vs Latency

Every policy sits somewhere on a Pareto frontier between:

  • Cost — proportional to active replicas (GPUs, pods, or nodes)
  • Latency — overload when arrival rate exceeds capacity, plus queue backlog and dropped work

Policies that minimize cost alone leave the cluster under-provisioned during spikes. Policies that chase minimum latency over-provision and waste budget.

Building the Simulator

In order to evaluate RL policies, a custom Gymnasium environment (AutoscalerEnv) that stands in for a Kubernetes-style inference fleet was needed. Training does not require a live cluster as all rollouts happen in simulation.

MDP formulation

ComponentDefinition
State (S_t)[λ_t, μ_t, N_t, δ_λ_t] — RPS, utilization, active replicas, RPS delta
Action (A_t)Discrete {0, 1, 2} → scale down, hold, scale up ({-1, 0, +1})
Reward (R_t)−(α·N_t + β·(overload + drops + γ·queue_depth))
  • α (cost_alpha): penalty per active replica
  • β (latency_beta): penalty for overload and dropped requests
  • γ (queue_penalty_gamma): extra weight on queue backlog

Maximum throughput per replica is μ (throughput_per_replica, default 50 RPS).

Realism: cold start and queue

Unlike a toy model with instant scale-up:

  1. Cold start — +1 enqueues a pod boot for cold_start_steps (default 3). Capacity does not increase until ready. Scale-down removes pending boots first, then active replicas (minimum 1).
  2. Queue — Arrivals plus backlog are served up to active_replicas × μ. Overflow beyond max_queue is dropped and penalized.

These dynamics are why a greedy policy with perfect next-step RPS still incurs large latency penalties on high-variance traces.

Adjust reward weights and a toy load scenario. Total reward R = −(α·N + β·overload + β·γ·queue).

Policy Training

With the environment and data handled, the next step is to train policies with Ray RLlib on parallel AutoscalerEnv rollouts. PPO (on-policy, stable updates) and DQN (off-policy, discrete actions) are compared against classical baselines.

Architecture

If you are interested in reproducing this yourself, this is the workflow I used with Ray RLlib and MLflow:

AutoscalerEnv  ◄── parallel env runners (Ray RLlib)
       │
       ▼
  PPO / DQN learner (2×256 MLP actor–critic)
       │
       ├── checkpoints/ppo/final, checkpoints/dqn/final
       └── MLflow (episode_return_mean, policy entropy)

Analysis

Why PPO beats greedy on return

Greedy minimizes instantaneous mismatch between RPS and capacity but does not account for cold-start delay: scaling up today does not add throughput for several timesteps. On the CSV replay, greedy accumulates a large latency penalty (17,468 mean) despite low replica cost (98).

PPO and DQN learn to anticipate bursts from state features (RPS, utilization, delta) and accept higher replica cost during volatile windows—reducing overload without a perfect oracle.

Ground-truth gap (oracle replicas)

Scaling plots compare active replicas to an ideal count derived from instantaneous RPS (no boot delay). Symmetric |ΔN| alone is misleading: underprovisioning (ideal > active) drives SLO risk; overprovisioning (active > ideal) mainly adds cost. On episode 0 (seed 42), mean gaps:

Why PPO overprovisions but DQN tracks ideal more closely

On the scaling figure, PPO (blue) often sits at max replicas after bursts and is slow to scale down (“delayed scale-down”), while DQN (purple) follows the black ideal line more closely—similar in spirit to Greedy (green), but with less underprovisioning at spikes.

This is not a training failure; the two algorithms learn different points on the same reward tradeoff:

  1. PPO minimizes latency first. With latency_beta much larger than replica cost (cost_alpha), PPO learns that missing capacity during cold-start is expensive. It scales up aggressively (122 scale-up vs 47 scale-down actions on this episode) and holds high replica counts through quiet periods so it never re-enters overload when traffic returns. That shows up as mean over ≈ 9.6 and mean under ≈ 0.05—almost no SLO risk, lots of extra GPUs.

  2. DQN scales down more often. On the same trace, DQN takes 84 scale-down steps vs PPO’s 47, so it releases capacity sooner when utilization falls. Replica counts stay closer to ideal (mean over ≈ 7.8, mean under ≈ 0.08), which looks better against ground truth but leaves slightly more headroom for queue buildup (latency 639 vs PPO’s 393).

  3. On-policy vs off-policy dynamics. PPO updates from the policy it just ran; once “scale up early, scale down late” works, the clipped objective reinforces that safe-but-expensive behavior. DQN’s replay and Q-learning spread credit across many past states, which tends to produce a less extreme replica level—closer to greedy in shape, but still with enough proactive scale-up to beat greedy on return.

Takeaway: PPO wins episode return (−692 vs −902) by paying for reliability (low underprovisioning). DQN wins visual fit to ideal by accepting a bit more SLO risk and less idle capacity. Neither metric alone tells the full story; compare return, latency/cost penalties, and asymmetric under/over gaps together.

PPO vs DQN (returns)

  • PPO (−692 return): better balance on this trace; higher cost (299) but much lower latency (393).
  • DQN (−902 return): slightly cheaper replicas (263) but more latency (639)—a different point on the cost–latency frontier.

For deployment, PPO is the stronger default from these benchmarks; DQN remains useful as an off-policy baseline and for ablations (learning rate, replay, etc.).

⚠️ Simulation ≠ production

Results are from a simulator with simplified dynamics. Phase 3 plans Ray Serve deployment and shadow evaluation against live metrics before any production recommendation.

Conclusion

We set out to learn scaling policies for ML inference that balance replica cost and latency under bursty traffic. The key ingredients were:

  1. A realistic simulator with cold starts, queueing, and trace-driven RPS
  2. A clear MDP reward with tunable α, β, and γ
  3. Distributed RL training via Ray RLlib (PPO and DQN)
  4. Rigorous benchmarks against classical baselines

On a held-out GPU-style trace, PPO dramatically outperformed fixed, HPA-style, and greedy policies in episode return—demonstrating that RL can learn temporal structure that myopic heuristics miss.
